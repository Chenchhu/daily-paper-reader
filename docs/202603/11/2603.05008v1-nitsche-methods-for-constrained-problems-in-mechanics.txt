Abstract 

We present guidelines for deriving new Nitsche Finite Element Methods to enforce equality and inequality constraints that act on the value of the un-known mechanical quality. We first formulate the problem as a stabilized finite element method for the saddle point formulation where a Lagrange multiplier enforces the underlying constraint. The Nitsche method is then presented in a general minimization form, suitable for nonlinear finite ele-ment methods and allowing straightforward computational implementation with automatic differentation. To validate these ideas, we present Nitsche formulations for a range of problems in solid mechanics and give numerical evidence of the convergence rates of the Nitsche method. 

Keywords: Nitsche method, finite element method, inequality constraints, contact problems, stabilization

Preprint submitted to International Journal of Engineering Science March 6, 2026 1. Introduction 

In the 1970s, Nitsche [1] introduced a method for enforcing the Dirich-let boundary conditions in the weak formulation of the finite element method. The benefits of the Nitsche method over its alternatives, the penalty method [2] and the mixed Lagrange multiplier method [3], include consistency, symmetry and positive definiteness, stability, and conditioning; cf. [4]. Hence, the ideas of Nitsche have later found applications also in domain decomposition [5], discontinuous Galerkin methods [6], unfitted finite element and fictitious do-main methods [7], Robin boundary conditions [8], Kirchhoff plates [9], elastic contact problems [10], friction problems [11], obstacle problems [12], elasto-plastic torsion problems [13], and plate contact problems [14], to name a few. In modern literature, the Nitsche method is often presented as a consis-tency correction to the penalty method, i.e., to make sure that the exact solution satisfies the discrete weak form, by including additional terms; cf., e.g., [15, 4]. This fails to answer to the obvious follow-up question: how to generalize the method to other problems and constraints? Consequently, we take a different viewpoint based on the saddle point formulation and present guidelines to derive optimal Nitsche methods for both equality and inequality constraints. These guiding principles apply to arbitrary constrained mini-mization problems, where the constraint acts on the values of the unknown. The resulting energy minimization form is particularly well-suited for solv-ing nonlinear problems numerically. We follow the guidelines in practice by deriving and implementing novel methods for problems such as inequality boundary condition for plates, membrane-solid contact problem, and plate-plate contact problem. Our formulation is based on a reinterpretation of the Nitsche method by Stenberg [16]. More precisely, on the idea that the Nitsche method corre-sponds to a conforming stabilized Lagrange multiplier method (in the sense of residual Barbosa–Hughes stabilization [17]). For such stabilized methods, an element-wise elimination of the Lagrange multiplier will lead to the equiv-alent Nitsche method; see also [10, 18, 19, 20]. This connection was exploited in the error analysis in Gustafsson [21] and consequently it became evident that the stabilized formulation leads to a family of methods with constraints of rather general form. Let us summarize the prior theoretical findings for existing Nitsche meth-ods: (1) stability becomes automatic if correct residuals of the Lagrange 2multiplier are included in the formulation; (2) optimal convergence rates are obtained if the scaling of the residual with respect to the mesh and the material parameters correctly mimics the continuous Sobolev norm of the Lagrange multiplier. In this paper, built on these observations, we explain how to define and use general Nitsche methods, present examples of their adaptation to novel physical problems, and show numerical evidence of their convergence. Amore careful numerical analysis, i.e., proofs of stability and error estimates, is beyond the scope of this work. The remainder of the paper is organized as follows. In Section 2, we review the original Nitsche method for the Dirichlet boundary condition u = g. In Section 3, we demonstrate how the method is modified to satisfy the inequality constraint u ≥ g. In Section 4, we describe a general form of the Nitsche method and how it can be used to derive new methods. In Section 5, we present some methods that exist in the literature and are of this general form. In Section 6, we derive novel Nitsche methods from the general form and show numerical evidence of their performance.

Hence, for new problems written in the general form (21), with energy 

J(uh) and finite element space Vh, it is necessary to define: 1. A constraint β = β(uh) ≥ 0 and the subset Γ ⊂ Ω where the constraint can become active. 2. An expression for the continuous Lagrange multiplier (i.e., the contact force) as λ = λ(uh).3. An expression for the parameter γ = γ(h, κ ).In order to find an expression for λ = λ(uh), it is necessary to derive the strong formulation of the continuous saddle point problem. This is usu-ally obtained by first deriving the variational formulation and then integrat-ing by parts to find the corresponding differential equations and boundary conditions, some of which will depend directly on λ. For contact-type con-straints between two domains, prior research suggests that the parameter γ

8and, hence, the expression for λ should be defined on the less stiff side; cf., e.g., [24]. Physically, equation (21) shows that an important role of γ is to scale the units of displacement in β to the units of force in λ. More precisely, λ should be proportional to βγ with the proportionality constant independent of h and 

κ. Therefore, the material parameters of the problem must be included in 

γ. Mathematically speaking, a correct scaling of γ is necessary to prove the uniform stability of the discrete saddle point formulation using an inverse inequality ; cf., e.g., [12, 14, 24]. Finally, we wish to remark that the penalty method can be recovered from this formulation by removing the Lagrange multiplier terms (formally, 

λ = 0 ), and that the Nitsche method for equality constraints is obtained simply by dropping the maximum operator (subscript +). The solution of the minimization problem (21) using Newton’s method is explained in Appendix A.

6. Novel methods 

We now present some novel methods based on formulation (20). More precisely, we consider the following problems: 1. Two-membrane contact problem, 2. Membrane against solid contact problem, 3. Plate against plate contact problem, 4. Kirchhoff plate with inequality boundary constraint. 10 For each problem, we present the Nitsche formulation and numerically in-vestigate its convergence rate. The numerical results are computed using scikit-fem [26] which is used to solve the nonlinear variational problems us-ing Newton’s method. For completeness, we describe how to manipulate the energy minimization problems for Newton’s method in Appendix Ap-pendix A. The programming implementation utilizes automatic differentia-tion with JAX [27] so that it is not necessary to manually calculate the func-tional derivatives involved in Newton’s method. The source code is available in [28] for reproducing the numerical results. Convergence rates are estimated via uniform mesh refinement. In the absence of an analytical solution, we estimate the convergence rate by eval-uating the error between two subsequent discrete solutions corresponding to mesh parameters 2h and h. Assuming that the error satisfies ∥u − uh∥ ≤ Ch p

7. Conclusions 

While the Nitsche method is commonly viewed as a consistency correction to the penalty method, we demonstrate how new methods can be obtained starting from a general minimization form. This allows for developing novel Nitsche methods for problems with both equality and inequality constraints in a simple manner. We derived and implemented several new problems using the approach. Our numerical experiments suggest that the methods converge optimally while the more careful numerical analysis remains a topic of future work.
